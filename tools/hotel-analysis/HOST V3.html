ASSESSMENT INSTRUCTIONS & GUIDELINES

◊
Assessment Overview

HOST is a hospitality security assessment toolkit. It evaluates physical security, electronic systems, emergency preparedness, and operational security measures to identify vulnerabilities and support practical improvement planning.

▤
Assessment Process

Complete each section systematically: Facility Information, Infrastructure & Dependencies, Physical Security, Security Systems, VIP Planning, and Emergency Planning. Each section builds upon the previous one to consider a comprehensive security profile.

◐
Time Requirements

Allow 1-2 hours for a complete assessment depending on facility size and complexity. The tool auto-saves your progress, allowing you to complete the assessment over multiple sessions.

📄
Required Documentation

Gather facility blueprints, security system specifications, emergency procedures, staff training records, vendor contracts, and contact information for key personnel before beginning the assessment.

👥
Assessment Team

Include security personnel, facilities management, emergency response coordinators, and hotel management in the assessment process to ensure comprehensive coverage of all security aspects.

💾
Data Management

Use the data management tools at the bottom of each page to save, load, validate, and export your assessment data. Regular saving ensures no data loss during the assessment process.

ELECTRIC VEHICLE CHARGING STATIONS

Document all EV charging stations and their specifications

Location Where this charger sits (garage level, surface lot, valet lane, etc.). Intent: tie electrical and life-safety context to a place. Why it matters: responders, towing, and patrol routes must find the asset; location drives exposure to weather, traffic, and unauthorized plug-in. Charger Type Connector family and tier (e.g., Level 2, DC fast). Intent: record what vehicles can use and how long sessions run. Why it matters: high-power DC equipment changes heat, cable management, and utility load—not just “there is a charger.” Power Level Delivered power in kW. Intent: document demand on circuits and transformers. Why it matters: undersized supply causes nuisance trips; oversized runs affect arc-flash and coordination with facility electrical maintenance. Access Type Who may start a charge—guest-only, pay-to-public, mixed. Intent: show whether strangers can enter the lot or queue. Why it matters: open public access changes after-hours patrol, queue disputes, and whether chargers become a loitering attractor. Site Suppression Whether fixed suppression (sprinklers, etc.) covers this charging zone per code and design. Intent: align with fire protection expectations for energized equipment. Why it matters: EV fires are resource-intensive; missing suppression affects pre-fire planning and mutual aid. FD Suppression Whether fire apparatus can reach and flow water or agent to this spot (aisle width, standpipes, FDC proximity). Intent: bridge site design to response reality. Why it matters: chargers tucked behind tight turns or deep in structures may delay knockdown even when on-site suppression exists. Actions

RESTRICTED ACCESS & VIP AREAS

Specify restricted areas (Floor/Area), access control, egress routes, and VSS coverage. These areas are secured using electronic access control devices—such as card readers, keypads or biometrics—integrated with the property management system.

Area Type What kind of restricted area is this (e.g., executive floor, VIP suite, IT, mechanical)? Intent: classify sensitivity and who may belong there. Why it matters: controls, visitor rules, and incident response differ sharply by area type. Location/Floor Where this area sits in the building. Intent: tie the row to a place responders and staff can find under stress. Why it matters: unclear location breaks lockdowns, escorts, and service coordination. Rooms How many rooms or spaces are inside this restricted area. Intent: scale of exposure and cleaning/security workload. Why it matters: larger enclaves need clearer sub-routes and more access-log review. Occupancy Typical or max occupancy for this area. Intent: size life-safety and evacuation assumptions. Why it matters: overcrowding changes queueing at controlled doors and time to clear the space. Access Control How people prove identity before entry (badge, biometric, key, PIN, staffed desk, etc.). Intent: document the real gate at the boundary. Why it matters: weak or shared credentials defeat “restricted” status and complicate audits after incidents. Elevator Access Whether elevators serving this area require credential or escort. Intent: show if the vertical path bypasses lobby screening. Why it matters: uncontrolled elevator floors are a common path for unauthorized movement to sensitive levels. Stairwell Exclusion Method How stair doors stay secured (crash bar, card reader, lock, alarm on propped, etc.). Intent: show how you stop lateral movement between floors. Why it matters: stairwells are often used in tandem with elevator tailgating; gaps here undermine floor isolation. VSS Coverage Level of video coverage for entries, corridors, and critical spots in this area. Intent: align detection and review with risk. Why it matters: without usable footage, investigations, visitor disputes, and post-incident reconstruction are much harder. Monitoring Type What else watches this area beyond fixed cameras—alarms, motion, guard tours, IDS tied to SOC, etc. Intent: show layered sensing and who responds. Why it matters: technology-only or guard-only coverage leaves predictable blind spots and slower confirmation. Actions

RETAIL & COMMERCIAL SPACES SECURITY ASSESSMENT

Assess security measures for all retail and commercial spaces within the hotel property, focusing on their ability to segregate from general public access during elevated risk periods. This includes restaurants, spas, gyms, boutiques, and other commercial considerations.

Space Name Name or brand of the retail/commercial space (restaurant, spa, shop, etc.). Intent: distinguish tenanted operations from hotel core. Why it matters: third-party venues often have their own hours, keys, and cash—your security model must name them explicitly. Location/Floor Where the space sits relative to public circulation. Intent: show adjacency to lobbies, streets, or guest corridors. Why it matters: spaces that open to the street or mall have different screening, after-hours, and protest/crowd risks. Entrance Type Whether guests enter from outside, only from inside the hotel, or both. Intent: count paths that need different hours, locks, and staff. Why it matters: exterior-only entrances need stronger night-time control; dual entrances complicate lockdowns. Door Lock Technology How doors are secured after hours—mechanical, ELS, shutters, alarms, etc. Intent: document what actually stops walk-in. Why it matters: propped or shared keys between tenant staff are a frequent gap when the public floor is “closed.” Open to Public Whether non-guests may enter during normal operations. Intent: separate hotel-only enclaves from open retail. Why it matters: public-facing spaces need clearer separation from guest floors during elevated events or threats. Management Hotel-operated vs. leased/tenant-operated. Intent: show who owns day-to-day access control and incident calls. Why it matters: tenants may not follow your SOC or key policies unless contractually aligned. Restrictable Access Whether the hotel can close or harden this space when risk rises (events, VIP, civil unrest). Intent: test operational flexibility. Why it matters: if you cannot segment retail from guest paths, crowd control options are limited. VSS Coverage How well video covers entry, POS, back-of-house, and queue lines. Intent: match cameras to theft, dispute, and safety review needs. Why it matters: retail incidents often hinge on whether cameras cover hands, faces, and exits—not just the door. Actions
